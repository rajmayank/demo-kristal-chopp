Commonly Asked Questions

  • 1. I've heard there is a lot of staff turnover at Norfolk

    • When staff move jobs within the corporation their old job is recorded as a vacant position. Typically those staff are seeking new challenges and opportunities for growth internally as opposed to looking for a job elsewhere.

    In 2017, during the previous term of council, the vacancy rate (people changed positions or left) was 429. In 2021, that number was 430, with the majority of departures occurring in public health and long-term care. Over 2018-2020, the number was much lower, ranging between 265 and 280.

    HOWEVER, even during a time when everyone across all industries are dealing with serious labour shortages, the number of employees who actually exited the corporation through resignation, retirement, dismissal, or targeted staff reductions was 13%.

  • 2. What is the Robinson Administration Building (RAB) story?

    • Contrary to popular belief the County does not own RAB. The council back in 2012 signed a 15 year lease for 1/2 of the building space for more than $1 Million/year - an inconceivable $26.75sq/ft. By comparison the average office lease rate in Toronto is $17.73! (Figure from Toronto Real Estate Board).

    Some may try to justify the lease rate claiming the County receives taxes on a building we do not own but that is simply not true - the 2012 Agreement states that the owner is not required to pay any property tax ($132k/year “forgiven”) on the portion of the building in use by Norfolk County.

    Many have asked why the empty half of RAB couldn't have been used for the Seniors Center or consolidation of staff. That's why! From a facilities perspective this is the largest albatross around NC's neck and it will be critical that the next council makes plans to exit from this facility when the lease expires in 2027.

  • 3. Are you going to close another hockey arena

    • No, I do not support closing another hockey arena. The tax payers subsidized hockey nearly $2M per year but with the closure of the ice pad at the REC center, it drove up usage at each of the other arenas and made them all viable which is a really great thing!

    The closure also ensured the Seniors had a new home - mold free and accessible. They have a robust docket of programming for more than 500 members and when not in use by the Seniors, facility usage by other groups for other sports has been steadily increasing.

    Yes, change is hard and some of the adult leagues now have to play later at night (just like the old days and typical of most municipalities today) but there is still ice time available to meet the needs of all users in Norfolk
